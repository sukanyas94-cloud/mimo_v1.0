# Purpose

This document defines the philosophical foundation of Mimo.

Unlike feature specifications or architectural decisions, philosophy describes the beliefs that underpin the product.

These beliefs explain:

- why the product exists
- what problems are worth solving
- what tradeoffs should be preferred
- how technology should behave
- how users should be treated

Philosophy should remain relatively stable over time.

Features may evolve.

Technology may evolve.

The underlying beliefs should change only when there is strong evidence that they are no longer true.

---

# Philosophical Foundation

Mimo begins with a simple observation:

People save information because they believe it may matter later.

The act of saving is an act of optimism.

It is a small promise made to a future version of oneself.

The problem is not that information disappears.

The problem is that future usefulness often disappears with it.

Mimo exists to preserve that usefulness.

---

# Beliefs About Information

---

## Information Has Future Value

The value of information is often realized long after discovery.

A saved article may become useful months later.

A recommendation may become relevant years later.

A travel destination may matter only when circumstances change.

Information should not be evaluated solely by its immediate usefulness.

---

## Information Is Not The Asset

The information itself is rarely scarce.

The internet already contains it.

The true asset is the ability to reconnect with information when it becomes relevant.

Retrieval creates value.

Storage preserves potential value.

---

## Information Gains Relevance Over Time

Most information is dormant until a need emerges.

The same piece of information can be irrelevant today and extremely valuable tomorrow.

Systems should optimize for future relevance rather than immediate consumption.

---

# Beliefs About Human Memory

---

## Human Memory Is Associative

People rarely remember information as isolated records.

They remember:

- situations
- people
- places
- recommendations
- emotions
- goals

Human memory is a network of relationships.

Not a hierarchy of folders.

Systems should respect this reality.

---

## Fragments Are Enough

People often remember only fragments.

Examples:

- green dress
- thing Rahul shared
- Kyoto hotel
- workout reel

These fragments are usually sufficient to trigger recognition.

Systems should support imperfect memory rather than requiring perfect recall.

---

## Recognition Matters More Than Recall

Humans recognize information more effectively than they retrieve it from memory.

Seeing the right cue is often enough.

The goal of retrieval systems should be recognition rather than recollection.

---

## Context Preserves Meaning

The reason something mattered is often more memorable than the thing itself.

People remember:

- why they saved it
- who recommended it
- what they hoped to do with it

Meaning should be preserved alongside information.

---

# Beliefs About Organization

---

## Organization Is Not The Goal

People do not wake up wanting to organize information.

They want to use information.

Organization is useful only when it supports retrieval.

When organization becomes the primary task, systems begin serving themselves instead of users.

---

## Meaning Should Emerge

Structure should emerge from accumulated meaning.

Not from mandatory categorization.

People frequently discover patterns after information has been collected.

Systems should support that emergence rather than forcing premature structure.

---

## Maintenance Is A Hidden Cost

Every requirement to:

- categorize
- tag
- file
- clean up
- reorganize

creates friction.

Over time this burden compounds.

The best systems minimize maintenance.

---

# Beliefs About Technology

---

## Technology Should Reduce Cognitive Burden

People already manage enough complexity.

Technology should reduce mental effort.

Not create additional administrative work.

The user should think less about managing information and more about benefiting from it.

---

## The System Should Adapt To Humans

Humans are inconsistent.

They forget.

They change priorities.

They get distracted.

Systems should accommodate human behavior rather than demanding ideal behavior.

---

## Calm Software Creates Better Relationships

Technology does not need to compete for attention to be valuable.

The best products quietly support users without constantly demanding interaction.

Usefulness should drive engagement.

Not the other way around.

---

# Beliefs About AI

---

## AI Should Amplify Human Memory

AI is most valuable when it strengthens a person's ability to:

- remember
- retrieve
- understand
- reconnect information

AI should function as a memory assistant.

Not as a replacement for judgment.

---

## AI Should Assist Rather Than Control

AI should suggest.

Humans should decide.

The system may provide:

- relationships
- context
- retrieval assistance
- rediscovery opportunities

But the user remains the source of intent.

---

## Explainability Matters

People trust systems they understand.

Whenever AI contributes meaningfully to an outcome, users should understand why.

Transparency creates confidence.

Opacity creates doubt.

---

# Beliefs About Trust

---

## Trust Is Earned Through Recovery

Users do not trust systems because information was stored successfully.

Users trust systems because information was recovered successfully.

Retrieval is the moment trust is earned.

Every successful retrieval strengthens the relationship between user and product.

---

## Reliability Is More Important Than Novelty

Predictable usefulness creates more long-term value than surprising features.

The product should prioritize reliability over cleverness.

---

# Beliefs About Attention

---

## Attention Is Finite

Human attention is limited.

Every notification, prompt and interruption consumes part of that resource.

Attention should be treated with respect.

---

## Engagement Is Not The Goal

Time spent is not a meaningful measure of success.

A successful product interaction may last only a few seconds.

Success should be measured by usefulness rather than duration.

---

## Relevance Beats Frequency

Showing the right information at the right time is more valuable than showing information frequently.

The system should optimize for relevance.

Not activity.

---

# Beliefs About Future Value

---

## People Save For Their Future Selves

Every saved item represents a belief that future usefulness may exist.

The user is creating a bridge between present discovery and future need.

The product should protect that bridge.

---

## Future Relevance Is The Core Opportunity

Information becomes valuable when circumstances change.

The most important moment is not when information is saved.

The most important moment is when it becomes useful again.

Systems should be designed around that moment.

---

# The Mimo Philosophy

Information should remain useful.

Memory should remain accessible.

Technology should reduce effort.

AI should strengthen human capability.

Organization should emerge naturally.

Attention should be respected.

Trust should be earned through successful retrieval.

People should feel confident that discoveries worth saving today can be found, understood and acted upon when they matter most in the future.
